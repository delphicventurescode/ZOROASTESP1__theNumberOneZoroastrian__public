## Chapter Two

## Malabar Hill, Suspicion, and Very Expensive Tea

The Contractor residence occupied a piece of Malabar Hill real estate large enough to trigger philosophical questions about inheritance tax.

"How many people live here?" asked K as they approached the gate.

"Usually?" said A. "Or legally?"

The watchman regarded them with the expression of a man who had spent thirty years observing wealthy families manufacture avoidable problems.

Inside, the house was magnificent.

It was also unmistakably inhabited.

Family photographs covered the walls. Silver-framed navjote portraits stretched back decades. Stern-looking ancestors stared down from oil paintings as though disappointed by modernity.

Mrs. Contractor met them in the drawing room.

"Everyone is here," she said.

"Excellent," replied A.

"Is it?"

"Well, maybe."

~

One by one, the suspects assembled.

First came Rustom Contractor, the boy's father.

An engineer by training, he possessed the exhausted appearance of a man who spent his life solving practical problems while surrounded by emotional ones.

Then came his wife, Shiraz. Sometimes, they called her Farah after Farah Pahlavi.

Calm. Intelligent. Watching everything.

The nephew arrived next.

Jamshed.

The cryptocurrency enthusiast.

K immediately recognized him.

"You have a podcast."

Jamshed looked pleased.

"I do."

A looked alarmed.

"You say that as though it were a qualification."

Finally came Meher Contractor's niece, Shernaaz.

A lawyer.

Which meant, A reflected, that the room now contained two professions universally distrusted by the public.

Lawyers and podcasters.

~

The tea arrived.

The tea alone could probably have financed a small startup.

A listened while Mrs. Contractor explained the disappearance.

The diamonds had been stored in a locked cabinet in the late Mr. Contractor's study.

The key remained in Meher's possession.

No sign of forced entry.

No signs of outsiders.

No evidence of burglary.

Only the diamonds were gone.

"Interesting," said A.

"What is?"

"The thief displayed restraint."

Everyone looked puzzled.

"Most thieves," said A, "lack discipline. They take everything. A selective thief usually has a specific purpose."

The room grew quiet.

A stood.

"I would like to see the study."

The late Mr. Contractor's study looked exactly as a successful Parsi industrialist's study ought to look.

Books.

Photographs.

Awards.

A large desk.

A globe no one had ever used for navigation.

The cabinet stood against the wall.

The lock showed no damage.

Inside lay several empty velvet compartments.

K examined them.

"Definitely missing."

"Insightful," said A.

"Thank you."

A ignored him.

Something else had caught his attention.

A framed photograph stood near the cabinet.

It showed a navjote ceremony from almost thirty years earlier.

The young boy in the picture wore the very diamond cufflinks now missing.

A picked it up.

Then another photograph.

And another.

The same diamonds appeared repeatedly.

Generation after generation.

The same heirloom.

The same ceremony.

The same family ritual.

A smiled.

Which worried K.

Whenever A smiled during an investigation, it usually meant somebody was about to have an unpleasant afternoon.

"What?" asked K.

A pointed toward the photographs.

"Observe."

"The diamonds?"

"No."

"The children?"

"No."

"The fashion?"

"Good heavens, no."

K frowned.

"I give up."

A placed the photographs on the desk.

"The person who took the diamonds does not care about jewellery."

"What makes you think that?"

"Because these photographs reveal what is actually valuable."

K stared.

Then understanding began to dawn.

"The tradition?"

"Exactly."

A nodded.

"Diamonds can be sold."

He tapped one of the photographs.

"But this?"

He tapped it again.

"This is leverage."

Back downstairs, the family waited nervously.

A resumed his seat.

"Tell me," he said, "which one of you recently had an argument about money?"

Nobody answered.

Which was answer enough.

A sighed.

Families, he reflected, were like criminal conspiracies.

The only difference was that criminal conspiracies tended to be better organized.
