# The Number 1 Zoroastrian Esperantist Detective Agency

## Story One: *The Case of the Missing Navjote Diamonds*

### Chapter One

### An Unpleasant Matter of Family Jewellery

Nobody had ever accused A of optimism.

“Hope,” he often said, stirring sugar into tea with unnecessary aggression, “is what people manufacture when evidence is unavailable.”

On damp Mumbai mornings, he sat in the office of **The Number 1 Zoroastrian Detective Agency**, directly above an Irani café whose proprietor claimed, with no supporting evidence, that three cabinet ministers and one famous smuggler owed him money.

The office had once belonged to a solicitor, then a dentist of questionable competence, then a charity devoted to preserving antique ceiling fans. A saw no reason to renovate.

Its single wooden sign hung at a mild angle:

**THE NUMBER 1 ZOROASTRIAN DETECTIVE AGENCY**
*Humata. Hukhta. Huvarshta.*
*Discreet Investigations.*

Nobody knew who had added “Number 1.”

A suspected K.

“Branding,” K would say. “Psychology. Nobody hires Number 3.”

K—who preferred, for reasons never satisfactorily explained, to be known simply as K—handled correspondence, research, tea procurement, online payments, and approximately eighty percent of unnecessary enthusiasm.

He was thirty years younger than A and spoke with the dangerous confidence of a man who had read half of several business books.

“You know what we need?” K announced one Tuesday morning.

“A profitable client?”

“Instagram.”

A looked up slowly.

“Mr. K,” he said gravely, “if I am ever photographed solving crimes for something called content, I want you to arrange a respectable funeral.”

The argument ended when footsteps approached the staircase.

Measured footsteps.

Expensive footsteps.

There exists, A maintained, a recognizable sound produced by inherited wealth attempting discretion.

A woman entered.

Not young. Not old. Elegant in the way South Bombay money becomes elegant when it no longer feels obliged to announce itself.

Her pearls were restrained.

Her anxiety was not.

“Mr. A?” she asked.

“Regrettably.”

She sat without invitation.

“My name is Meher Contractor,” she said. “I was told you handle sensitive matters involving Parsi families.”

“That depends,” said A. “If somebody has insulted somebody at a wedding, we charge double.”

She did not smile.

“My grandson’s navjote is in twelve days.”

A nodded.

This, on the surface, did not appear criminal.

“In our family,” she continued carefully, “there is a tradition. For five generations, the eldest grandson receives a ceremonial set of diamond cufflinks and a small diamond pin at his navjote.”

K leaned forward.

“Family heirlooms?”

“Yes.”

“And now they’re missing?” asked A.

Her face tightened.

“They have disappeared.”

A waited.

People often revealed the important facts only after silence had cornered them.

“They were kept,” she continued, “in a locked cabinet in my late husband’s study at Malabar Hill.”

“Who had access?”

“My son. My daughter-in-law. Domestic staff. My niece. And…”

She hesitated.

“Yes?” asked A.

“My nephew. Though I would rather he did not.”

“A family favourite?”

“A cryptocurrency enthusiast.”

K winced.

A folded his hands.

“When were the diamonds last seen?”

“Three Sundays ago.”

“And when did you discover them missing?”

“Yesterday.”

“Why yesterday?”

“Because,” she said quietly, “someone sent me an anonymous message.”

K’s eyebrows rose.

“What message?” he asked.

She removed her phone.

The text was brief.

**Some family traditions are too expensive to survive. Ask the boy’s father where the diamonds went.**

A read it twice.

Then handed the phone to K.

Outside, rain tapped against old Bombay windows.

Somewhere below, cups struck saucers in the café.

A disliked anonymous messages.

Anonymous messages usually meant one of three things:

Cowardice.

Manipulation.

Or family.

Especially family.

“Mrs. Contractor,” he said, reaching for his notebook, “before we begin, I must ask an important question.”

She looked worried.

“Has anyone,” A said, “already accused the wrong person?”

She looked away.

“My nephew.”

“Excellent,” A sighed. “Then we are already behind schedule.”
